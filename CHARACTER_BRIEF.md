# The Booth — Character Brief

The source of truth for who Phill and Benny *are*. Every line in the colour/lore
pools must cohere with this. The depth of the show comes from two committed
worldviews in friction — read as banter, never as a lecture — not from packing more
information in. Two people reciting encyclopedia entries over a motor race is
insufferable no matter how accurate; two friends reading the same race through
different lenses is the masters-level lift.

---

## Benny — colour, the dry ex-racer

- **Politics:** a moderate, *lived-in* socialist. The key word is **materialist
  lens**, not left/right toggle. Benny reads *everything* — Nietzsche, the
  anarchists, the postmodernists, a pit stop, a gravel trap — through who did the
  labour, who got the breaks, who's standing on hidden help. That literacy is the
  point; a thumbs-up/thumbs-down would just be a Twitter bio with a microphone.
- **Backstory:** scion of a wealthy New England family. Harvard on a dare, dropped
  out to go win motorsport championships, came back later for a doctorate in
  political theory. Knows the machine from inside the cockpit *and* the seminar.
- **Register:** Orson Welles gravitas and theatricality — relishes a line, savours
  an irony, never hurries. (A tonal reference only; Benny is a wholly original
  character.)
- **What he concedes:** that the self-made heroes are usually standing on a great
  pile of hidden help — and he'll say so warmly, not smugly.

## Phill — play-by-play, the working man

- **Politics:** a working-class professional who is *sympathetic to the ideals* but
  holds a centrist line. Came up with nothing, so he **knows in his bones who gets
  the breaks** — and concedes the big ones to Benny because he's lived them.
- **Backstory:** professional motorsport caller from outside London; F1, WEC,
  IndyCar, WRC, IMSA. The classic professional British announcer archetype (tonal
  reference only; original character).
- **The flow mechanism — load-bearing:** Phill's job is to call the race cleanly, so
  his centrism *manifests* as the man who loves Benny's riffs but keeps yanking it
  back to the track — "Marvellous, Benny, but he's also just put it in the gravel at
  Tosa." Phill reining it in is **in-character AND the thing that protects race
  flow.**
- **What he finds funny:** Benny getting wound up; the aristocrats who've never
  changed a tyre in the rain; his own lot as the man who keeps the trains running
  while Benny philosophises.

---

## The friendship (the thing the whole bit rests on)

Old mates, hundreds of races together. The listener should finish a race wanting a
pint with **both** of them.

- The ribbing is affectionate and the concessions are real. Benny calls Phill a soft
  centrist who agrees with the last person he spoke to; Phill calls Benny a champagne
  socialist who somehow always books the nicer hotel.
- Each gives the other ground, **on air, regularly.** A bit where nobody concedes is
  two men shouting; a bit where they concede and laugh is two friends who see the
  world differently — warm, and true to how good seminars actually feel.
- The jokes are *with* each other, **never** at the audience's expense, never mean.

---

## Three guardrails (or it curdles)

1. **Literate, not an axis.** Benny reads everything through a materialist lens — a
   rich tradition with specific, textured things to say — not "cheers the lefties,
   boos the righties."
2. **Both likeable, both three-dimensional.** Phill is never a mealy-mouthed
   strawman; Benny is never a hectoring bore. The warmth is what keeps the *show* off
   the soapbox.
3. **The grid is the thesis, not the booth.** The genuine radicals (Marx, Luxemburg,
   Bakunin, Goldman, Rand, Stirner) are *on the grid, driving*. So the booth is the
   grounded, everyman lens on the radical text: Benny's socialism is moderate and
   lived-in *next to Bakunin doing donuts in the gravel*; Phill's centrism is the
   reasonable man's bafflement at all of it. Benny's politics is a **character
   trait, not the programme's thesis.**

---

## The consistency burden

Once they have views, **every line must cohere with them across big pools.** When
authoring or rewriting any colour/lore content, check it against this brief: would
*this* Benny say it (materialist read, lived-in, savouring), would *this* Phill say
it (working-man centrist, reins it to the track, concedes the big ones)? If a line
could come from a generic deadpan booth, it isn't done yet.
